Home / Signal / Customer acquisition spread is 23x
Signal

We compared what 11 local businesses pay per customer. The spread is 23x.

One pays $4 per customer. Another pays $94. Same neighbourhoods. Same quality. The difference? Systems, automation, and knowing your numbers.

R Reese
March 7, 2026 Issue No. 009

I spent the last six weeks doing something slightly obsessive. I pulled the actual customer acquisition cost — not the estimated, not the “feels about right,” the actual CAC — from 11 local businesses in the same metro area.

Restaurants. Salons. A dental clinic. Two trades businesses. A physiotherapy practice. A med spa. A pet groomer. A yoga studio. A barbershop. And a nail salon.

Same city. Overlapping neighbourhoods. Comparable quality. Similar price points within their categories.

The results made me put my laptop down and stare at the wall for about ten minutes.

$4
lowest cost per new customer
$94
highest cost per new customer

Four dollars. Ninety-four dollars. For the same outcome — a new customer walking through the door. The spread between the best and worst was 23x.

Not 2x. Not 5x. Twenty-three times.

Let me break down why, because this isn’t a story about luck or location. It’s a story about systems versus vibes.

The $4 business: a barbershop running like a tech startup

The lowest CAC belonged to a barbershop. Not a trendy one with $60 cuts and an espresso machine. A neighbourhood barbershop with four chairs and a guy named Danny who’s been cutting hair for 19 years.

Danny doesn’t run Facebook ads. He doesn’t post on Instagram. What Danny does have is a system so tight it would make a SaaS founder jealous.

Every customer who sits in his chair gets a text 24 hours later: “Thanks for coming in. If you book your next cut right now, you skip the wait.” That single automated text drives a 74% rebooking rate. Three out of four customers book again before they even need a haircut.

The cheapest customer you’ll ever acquire is the one who’s already sitting in your chair. Danny figured that out with a $29/month text tool and zero marketing budget.

Then there’s the referral trigger. Every fifth visit, Danny’s system sends an automated message: “You’ve been coming here for a while. Know someone who needs a barber? Send them this link and you both get $5 off.” Simple. No app. No loyalty card. Just a text with a link.

Danny’s monthly marketing spend: $29 for the text platform. That’s it. His average new customer cost, fully loaded, works out to $4.12.

The $94 business: a salon spending blind

On the other end? A salon. Beautiful space. Talented stylists. Great reviews. And a customer acquisition cost that was quietly bleeding them dry.

23x
spread between lowest and highest CAC

The owner was spending $2,800 a month on Instagram ads, $600 on a social media manager, and another $400 on boosted posts. Total monthly marketing budget: $3,800. Sounds aggressive? It is. But here’s the problem: she had no idea which of those dollars were working.

No tracking links. No UTM parameters. No way to connect a specific ad to a specific booking. She was measuring success by follower count and “vibes.” When we actually traced her new customer flow, she was acquiring roughly 40 new clients a month. $3,800 ÷ 40 = $94 per head.

Meanwhile, her rebooking rate was 31%. Which means for every 10 new clients she paid $94 to acquire, 7 of them never came back. She was pouring money into a bucket with a hole in the bottom.

The most expensive customer isn’t the one that costs $94 to acquire. It’s the one that costs $94 to acquire and never comes back.

The pattern across all 11

Here’s what I found when I laid all 11 businesses side by side. The cost per customer had almost nothing to do with industry, location, or how much they spent on marketing. The pattern was simpler and more brutal than that.

The businesses with low CACs shared three traits:

1. They measured. Not vibes. Not follower counts. Actual numbers. How many new customers this month. What it cost to get them. What percentage came back. If you can’t answer those three questions about your own business right now, you’re flying blind.

2. They automated retention. Rebooking texts. Follow-up emails. Review requests. Birthday offers. None of this is rocket science. All of it is automated. The low-CAC businesses had some form of post-visit automation running. The high-CAC businesses had none.

3. They treated referrals as a system, not a hope. The low-CAC businesses had actual referral mechanisms — triggered texts, specific incentives, trackable links. The high-CAC businesses “hoped” happy customers would tell their friends. Hope is not a strategy.

businesses compared across one metro area

The uncomfortable math

Let me make this concrete. Say you’re a salon doing 50 new customers a month at $94 each. That’s $4,700 a month in acquisition costs — $56,400 a year — to fill chairs that could be filled at a fraction of the price.

If you dropped your CAC to even $20 — not $4, just $20 — you’d save $44,400 a year. That’s a full-time employee. That’s a renovation. That’s breathing room.

And the path from $94 to $20 isn’t more ads. It isn’t a better logo. It isn’t a viral TikTok. It’s a $29/month text platform, an automated rebooking sequence, and a referral trigger that runs while you sleep.

What this actually means

The gap I found across these 11 businesses isn’t a marketing gap. It’s a systems gap.

The expensive businesses are working harder, spending more, and getting less. The cheap businesses built small, quiet systems that compound over time. They’re not smarter. They’re not luckier. They just built the plumbing once and let it run.

If you own a local business and you don’t know your CAC, that’s the first thing to fix. Not your ads. Not your website. Your numbers. Because right now, there’s probably a business three blocks from you, in the same industry, paying a fraction of what you pay for every new customer.

And the gap is only getting wider.
