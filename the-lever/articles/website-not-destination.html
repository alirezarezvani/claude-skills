Your website might not be the thing people look at anymore

We pulled click data from 30 local businesses. The results will make you rethink where you spend your next dollar.

R Reese
March 7, 2026 Issue No. 009

I need to show you a number that might ruin your afternoon.

Last month, I pulled engagement data from 30 local businesses we work with — restaurants, salons, clinics, a couple of trades companies. I wanted to answer a simple question: where are customers actually interacting with these businesses online?

The answer broke my brain a little.

22%
of total customer engagement happens on the website
51%
happens on Google Business Profile

Read those numbers again. More than half of all customer engagement — views, clicks, calls, direction requests, photo views — is happening on Google Business Profile. Not the website. Not Instagram. Not Facebook. Google.

The remaining 27% was split across social media, review platforms, and direct search. The website — the thing most business owners spend $3,000–$10,000 redesigning every two years — captured barely a fifth of total engagement.

“Your website is not your front door anymore. Your Google listing is.”

The $5,000 redesign nobody sees

I know this is going to make some web designers angry, and I want to be clear: your website still matters. It’s your credibility anchor. When someone hears about you from a friend, they might check your site. When someone wants to see your full menu or service list, they’ll visit. It’s not useless.

But it’s not the first thing people see. And for most local businesses, it’s not even the second thing.

Here’s the customer journey I see over and over in the data: someone searches “Thai restaurant near me” or “hair salon downtown.” They see the Map Pack. They look at photos, reviews, and hours — all on Google. They click “call” or “directions” — directly from Google. They never visit the website at all.

The decision was made before they ever saw your homepage.

Where the money should go

If you’re a local business owner and you’re about to spend $5,000 on a website redesign, I want you to ask yourself one question first: when was the last time you updated your Google Business Profile?

Because that profile — the one getting 51% of your customer engagement — probably has photos from 2023, a description that reads like it was written by a robot in 2021, and zero posts in the last six months.

Here’s what I’d do with that $5,000 instead:

$0 — Update your GBP yourself. New photos (taken this week, on your phone). A complete, compelling description. Correct hours, including holiday hours. Answer every Q&A. Respond to every review. Post once a week. This is free. It takes 30 minutes a week. And it affects the platform where 51% of your customers are making decisions.

$500 — Professional photos. Hire a photographer for two hours. Get 40–50 high-quality shots of your space, your team, your food or services. Upload them to GBP, not just your website.

$1,500 — GBP optimization + local SEO. Get someone who knows what they’re doing to audit your profile, fix your categories, optimize for the keywords people actually search, and build out your local content strategy.

$3,000 — Keep for Google Ads. Targeted ads that show up when someone in your zip code searches for what you sell. Direct ROI you can measure.

That leaves $0 for the website redesign. And I’m okay with that. Because the website that’s “a little dated” but has a fully optimized GBP will outperform the gorgeous website with a neglected Google listing every single time.

The shift is already here

This isn’t a prediction. This is what the data already shows. The businesses in our dataset that invested in their GBP over their website saw 3.2x more customer actions (calls, direction requests, bookings) than those who did the opposite.

Your front door moved. Most business owners just haven’t noticed yet.

The ones who figure it out first? They’re the ones picking up the phone while their competitors are still arguing about font choices on a homepage nobody visits.
