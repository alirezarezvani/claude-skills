Field Notes

A salon owner told me she “doesn’t do marketing.” She filled 40 empty chairs in 3 weeks.

No ads. No social media. Just automated booking reminders, a rebooking prompt, and a referral text. $0 ad spend. 127% rebooking rate increase.

K Kyle
March 7, 2026 Issue No. 009

Priya said it to me with complete conviction, arms crossed, standing behind the front desk of her salon like she was defending a philosophical position.

“I don’t do marketing.”

She said it the way people say “I don’t eat gluten” — not as an observation, but as an identity. Priya owned a 6-chair salon in a busy suburb. Good stylists. Loyal regulars. Decent Google reviews. And 40 empty appointment slots per week.

Forty.

40
empty appointment slots per week

I didn’t find that number by spying. Priya told me herself, almost casually, like it was weather. “Yeah, we probably have 30, 40 open slots a week. It’s just how salons are. You can’t fill every chair every hour.”

Except you can. And we did. In three weeks.

The problem wasn’t demand

Here’s the thing that Priya — and honestly, most salon owners I’ve talked to — didn’t see. She didn’t have a demand problem. She had a follow-through problem.

Her stylists were good. Her clients liked coming. But after a visit, the relationship just… stopped. A client would get a great cut, walk out happy, and then… nothing. No reminder. No rebooking prompt. No follow-up. They’d think about booking again in 6 weeks, then forget. Then it’d be 8 weeks. Then 10. Then they’d walk past a new salon and think “maybe I’ll try that one.”

Not because Priya’s salon was bad. Because nobody asked them to come back.

The clients weren’t leaving because they were unhappy. They were leaving because nobody reminded them to stay.

Three systems. Zero ads. Three weeks.

I asked Priya for three weeks. No ads. No Instagram strategy. No marketing — at least not in the way she meant it. Just three small systems running quietly in the background.

System 1: The 24-hour rebooking prompt.

Twenty-four hours after every appointment, the client gets a text. Not a sales pitch. Not a coupon. Just: “Thanks for coming in yesterday! Want to lock in your next appointment? Tap here to book.” One link. One tap. Done.

Before this system, Priya’s rebooking rate — the percentage of clients who book their next appointment within 48 hours of their visit — was 22%.

127%
increase in rebooking rate (22% to 50%)

After two weeks with the automated text? 50%. That’s a 127% increase. Half of all clients were now rebooking before they’d even washed their hair at home. The text took 4 minutes to set up. It runs automatically. Priya doesn’t touch it.

System 2: The no-show rescue sequence.

Priya’s no-show rate was about 12%. That’s not unusual for salons, but it’s expensive. Every no-show is a stylist standing idle and revenue evaporating.

We set up a three-text sequence: a reminder 48 hours before the appointment, another the morning of, and — here’s the key one — a text 15 minutes after a missed appointment: “We missed you today! Want to reschedule? Here are our next available slots.”

The no-show rate dropped from 12% to 4%. And of the no-shows that remained, 60% rebooked from the rescue text. Those were appointments that previously would have been pure loss.

System 3: The referral trigger.

This was the one Priya was most sceptical about. “My clients don’t want to be bothered with referral stuff,” she told me. I asked her to give it two weeks.

After a client’s third visit, they get a text: “You’re officially one of our regulars! Know someone who needs a great stylist? Send them this link — they’ll get $15 off their first visit and you’ll get $15 off your next one.”

$0
spent on advertising

In the first three weeks, that referral text generated 23 new client bookings. Twenty-three people who walked in because a friend sent them a link. Priya’s cost to acquire those clients? The $15 discount. That’s it. No ad spend. No agency fees. No social media strategy.

“I told you I don’t do marketing,” Priya said, looking at the numbers. “Apparently I just didn’t know what marketing was.”

The 40 chairs

Let me count it up.

The rebooking texts converted clients who would have drifted into clients who locked in their next appointment immediately. That filled approximately 18 previously empty slots per week.

The no-show rescue sequence recovered appointments that would have been lost. That filled another 8–10 slots per week.

The referral triggers brought in brand-new clients. That filled another 12–15 slots per week.

Total: 40 chairs filled. In three weeks. With zero ad spend and three automated text sequences that took less than an hour to set up.

3 weeks
to fill 40 empty chairs per week

The real lesson

Priya wasn’t wrong when she said she doesn’t do marketing. She didn’t. And that was the problem.

But the solution wasn’t what she feared. It wasn’t Facebook ads and Instagram reels and dancing on TikTok. It wasn’t hiring a social media manager or paying an agency $2,000 a month to post pretty pictures of balayage.

It was three text messages. Automated. Triggered by events that were already happening. Running silently behind a business that was already good — just leaking customers out the back door because nobody thought to close it.

Most salon owners I talk to have the same gap. They’re great at the chair. They’re great at the craft. They’re just not great at the follow-up. And in 2026, the follow-up is where the money lives.

Priya still says she doesn’t do marketing. She just smiles when she says it now.
