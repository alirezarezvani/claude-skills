Field Notes

A chef said goodbye. His restaurant sold out for three straight weeks.

21 consecutive sold-out nights. $0 in ad spend. The entire campaign was 3 emails and 4 Instagram posts.

K Kyle
March 7, 2026 Issue No. 009

Marcus had been the head chef at Verdana for twelve years. When he told the owner he was leaving to open his own place, they had three weeks before his last service.

The owner, Jess, called me in a mild panic. “We need to do something. Marcus is this restaurant for a lot of people.”

She was right. And the “something” turned out to be simpler than either of us expected.

The campaign that wasn’t really a campaign

We sent three emails to their list of about 2,400 subscribers. The first was a letter from Jess — honest, personal, a little emotional. “Marcus is leaving. Here’s what he meant to this place. Come say goodbye.”

That email had a 68% open rate. Their usual average? 22%.

The second email, a week later, featured Marcus talking about his favorite dishes — the ones he’d put on a special farewell menu. The third, three days before his last night, was just: “Last chance. Friday is his final service.”

On Instagram, four posts. A photo of Marcus from his first day. A video of him plating his signature dish. A carousel of regulars’ messages. And a final “tonight’s the night” story.

21
consecutive sold-out nights

They sold out every single night for three weeks. Not just Fridays and Saturdays — Tuesdays, Wednesdays, the dead nights that usually had empty four-tops.

$0
spent on advertising
People don’t respond to marketing. They respond to moments that feel like they matter. A goodbye is a moment that matters.

Why this worked

It wasn’t the emails. It wasn’t the Instagram posts. It was the story. A real person, leaving a real place, after twelve real years. That’s not content strategy — it’s something people actually care about.

The lesson isn’t “stage a departure.” That would be cynical and people would smell it. The lesson is: your business is full of stories that people would respond to if you told them. The new hire who moved across the country. The recipe that took two years to perfect. The regular who’s been coming every Thursday for a decade.

Most businesses never tell these stories. They post food photos and hope for likes. Meanwhile, the stuff that would actually fill seats is happening right in front of them — unrecorded, unshared, unnoticed.

Marcus left. Verdana is still full on Fridays. Jess tells me it’s because those three weeks reminded her what her customers actually wanted: to feel like they were part of something.
